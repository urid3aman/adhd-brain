Coping Techniques

Structured strategies, mapped to real triggers

Each technique below is presented with its upstream trigger — the graph node it connects to. This isn't a random tips list. It's a dependency-mapped coping toolkit.

Body Doubling

upstream: task_initiation_failure → activation_energy_deficit

Work alongside someone (in person or virtually) to borrow external accountability. The presence of another person provides the activation energy your brain struggles to generate alone. Evidence suggests this reduces task-start latency significantly for ADHD individuals.

Time Blocking with Buffer Zones

upstream: time_blindness → schedule_collapse

Schedule tasks in blocks with 15-minute buffers between them. ADHD brains underestimate task duration (time blindness). Buffers absorb the overflow so one late task doesn't cascade into the entire day. Think of it as adding slack to your dependency graph.

Externalize Working Memory

upstream: working_memory_overload → thought_loss

Use a whiteboard, voice recorder, or app to immediately capture every thought. ADHD working memory is like RAM that randomly clears — externalization is your disk write. The goal: no thought lives only in your head.

Transition Rituals

upstream: hyperfocus_lock → task_switching_failure

Create a 2-minute ritual between tasks: stand up, drink water, name the next task aloud. Hyperfocus makes your brain resist switching. A physical ritual breaks the neurological "lock" and signals your brain that a context switch is happening.

Rejection Sensitivity First Aid

upstream: rejection_perceived → emotional_flooding

When you feel the sting: pause, label it ("This is RSD, not reality"), and delay any response by 20 minutes. Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD) is common in ADHD — the emotional flood feels urgent but almost always passes. The graph shows: RSD → rumination → withdrawal → isolation. Break it at step one.

Interest-Based Task Pairing

upstream: low_dopamine → motivation_deficit

Pair a boring task with something stimulating: do admin work while listening to a podcast, fold laundry during a show. ADHD motivation runs on interest, not importance. Pairing hacks your dopamine system by stacking a high-interest node onto a low-interest one.

The "Good Enough" Rule

upstream: perfectionism → task_paralysis

Set an explicit "done" threshold before starting: "This email is done when it has 3 sentences." ADHD perfectionism isn't about high standards — it's about not knowing when to stop. A pre-set threshold is a graph termination condition.

Medication Timing Awareness

upstream: medication_schedule → focus_window

Map your medication's peak effect window and schedule your hardest tasks there. If stimulant medication peaks at 10am–1pm, that's your high-performance graph node. Schedule creative/demanding work inside it, routine work outside it.

Mindfulness

ADHD-friendly mindfulness (not "just sit still")

Traditional meditation tells you to clear your mind. ADHD brains can't — and that's okay. These techniques work with your wiring, not against it.

🚶

Walking Meditation

Move and observe. Count steps in groups of 10. Your body stays stimulated (meeting ADHD's movement need) while your mind practices focused attention. Research by Zylowska et al. (2008) showed mindfulness adapted for ADHD improved attention and executive function.

Ref: Zylowska, 2008 — UCLA ADHD Mindfulness Study
🎵

Sound Anchoring

Pick one sound in your environment and follow it. The hum of a fan, distant traffic, birdsong. When your mind wanders (it will — that's ADHD), gently return to the sound. No judgment. The return IS the practice.

Works because ADHD brains anchor to stimuli
✋

5-4-3-2-1 Grounding

Name: 5 things you see, 4 you touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste. This sensory scan pulls an ADHD brain out of rumination by flooding it with present-moment input. Especially effective during RSD or anxiety spirals.

Immediate re-grounding for emotional flooding
🧘

Micro-Meditations (60 Seconds)

One minute. Three deep breaths. Name your current emotion. That's it. ADHD brains resist long meditation because the reward is too distant. 60-second micro-sessions stack over time and build the same neural pathways without the resistance.

Ref: Mitchell et al., 2017 — Brief Mindfulness for ADHD
📝

Thought Labeling

When a thought pops up, label its type: "planning," "worrying," "remembering," "creating." Don't judge or follow it — just tag it like a graph node. This builds metacognitive awareness, which is the ADHD brain's weakest and most improvable skill.

Graph your thoughts in real time
💨

Box Breathing with Fidget

Breathe in 4 counts, hold 4, out 4, hold 4 — while using a fidget toy. The fidget satisfies your brain's stimulation need so the breathing exercise doesn't feel like torture. Both hands and lungs engaged = full-body regulation.

Combines somatic + respiratory regulation

Nutrition & Exercise

What you eat and how you move changes how your brain works

These aren't vague wellness tips. Each recommendation below has research backing specifically for ADHD — showing how nutrition and movement connect to focus, impulse control, and emotional regulation.

🏃

Aerobic Exercise (30 min, 4x/week)

Evidence: Strong — Mehren et al. (2020), Frontiers in Psychiatry

Aerobic exercise increases dopamine and norepinephrine — the same neurotransmitters targeted by ADHD medications. A meta-analysis found that regular aerobic activity improved attention, executive function, and impulse control in both children and adults with ADHD. The effect is strongest when exercise is consistent, not occasional. Think of it as a daily "medication boost" node in your graph.

🥚

Protein at Breakfast

Evidence: Moderate — Wurtman & Wurtman, MIT amino acid research

Protein provides tyrosine and phenylalanine — amino acid precursors to dopamine. Starting the day with protein (eggs, Greek yogurt, nuts) stabilizes blood sugar and provides raw materials for neurotransmitter production throughout the morning. A carb-heavy breakfast → blood sugar spike → crash → afternoon focus collapse. The graph path is direct.

🐟

Omega-3 Fatty Acids

Evidence: Moderate — Chang et al. (2018), meta-analysis in Neuropsychopharmacology

A meta-analysis of 16 randomized controlled trials found omega-3 supplementation (particularly EPA at doses >500mg/day) showed small but significant improvements in ADHD symptoms, especially inattention. Not a replacement for medication, but a supporting node. Sources: fatty fish (salmon, sardines), walnuts, flaxseed, or supplements.

🏋️

Resistance Training

Evidence: Emerging — Vysniauske et al. (2020), J Attention Disorders

Weight training and bodyweight exercises improve executive function through a different pathway than cardio. The focus required during lifting acts as a mindfulness practice (you have to pay attention), while the progressive overload structure gives ADHD brains clear, measurable goals — a built-in reward loop.

🫐

Blood Sugar Stability

Evidence: Moderate — Johnson et al., elimination diet studies

ADHD brains are extra-sensitive to blood sugar fluctuations. Spikes and crashes amplify inattention, irritability, and impulsivity. Eating smaller meals with protein + complex carbs + healthy fats every 3–4 hours keeps the fuel line steady. The graph path: blood sugar crash → dopamine dip → focus collapse → frustration.

💧

Hydration

Evidence: General — mild dehydration impairs cognition across all populations

Even mild dehydration (1–2% body weight loss) impairs working memory and attention in clinical studies. ADHD brains are already running on reduced executive function bandwidth — dehydration taxes it further. Simple intervention: keep water visible and accessible. If it's out of sight, an ADHD brain forgets it exists.

Medication Guide

Understanding your options — so you can talk to your doctor informed

This is educational information, not medical advice. Medication decisions should always be made with a qualified healthcare provider. The goal here: give you the vocabulary and framework so those conversations are productive.

Methylphenidate (Ritalin, Concerta)

Stimulant — First-line treatment

Blocks reuptake of dopamine and norepinephrine, increasing their availability in the prefrontal cortex. Available in short-acting (3–4 hrs), intermediate (6–8 hrs), and extended-release (10–12 hrs) formulations. Most widely studied ADHD medication with decades of safety data.

Graph node: medication_schedule → focus_window. Map your peak effect hours.

Amphetamine-based (Adderall, Vyvanse)

Stimulant — First-line treatment

Increases release of dopamine and norepinephrine (slightly different mechanism than methylphenidate). Vyvanse (lisdexamfetamine) is a prodrug — inactive until metabolized, providing smoother onset and lower abuse potential. Some individuals respond better to amphetamine-based than methylphenidate-based, and vice versa.

If one stimulant class doesn't work, the other often does. ~70% respond to the first; ~90% to one of the two.

Atomoxetine (Strattera)

Non-stimulant — Norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor

Takes 4–6 weeks for full effect (unlike stimulants which work day one). Provides 24-hour coverage without the peaks and valleys of stimulants. Often chosen when stimulants cause anxiety, tics, or when there are substance abuse concerns. Less effective for inattention than stimulants but better tolerated by some.

No "medication wearing off" crash — the graph node is active 24/7.

Guanfacine / Clonidine (Intuniv, Kapvay)

Non-stimulant — Alpha-2 agonist

Originally blood pressure medications, now FDA-approved for ADHD. Particularly helpful for hyperactivity, impulsivity, and emotional dysregulation. Often used as an add-on to stimulants rather than standalone. Guanfacine extended-release (Intuniv) is more commonly used for ADHD than clonidine.

Especially useful if RSD or emotional flooding is a major graph node for you.

Bupropion (Wellbutrin)

Off-label — Dopamine/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor

Not FDA-approved for ADHD but frequently prescribed off-label, especially when ADHD co-occurs with depression. Acts on the same neurotransmitter systems as ADHD medications. Weaker effect on ADHD symptoms than stimulants, but addresses comorbid depression simultaneously.

Consider when your graph has strong depression ↔ ADHD bidirectional edges.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

Preparation — Make the appointment count

1. "What class of medication are you recommending and why?"
2. "What's the expected timeline to see effects?"
3. "How will we know if the dose needs adjusting?"
4. "What side effects should I watch for in the first 2 weeks?"
5. "How does this interact with anything else I take?"
6. "What does 'medication wearing off' look like, and how do we handle it?"

Write these down. ADHD working memory will blank them in the appointment.

Sources: Faraone et al. (2015) — meta-analysis of ADHD medications, The Lancet Psychiatry; NICE Guidelines NG87 (2018); Barkley (2015) — ADHD Handbook 4th ed. Always consult your prescribing physician.

Masking & Compensating

Women with ADHD develop elaborate systems to appear "put together" — meticulous lists, excessive preparation, people-pleasing. This masking costs enormous energy and often leads to burnout. The graph shows: masking → energy depletion → breakdown → "she seemed fine until she wasn't."

Ref: Hinshaw et al., 2022 — Berkeley Girls with ADHD Study
🌊

Hormonal Interactions

Estrogen modulates dopamine activity. During menstruation, perimenopause, and postpartum, estrogen drops — and ADHD symptoms intensify. Many women report their medication "stops working" at certain cycle points. This is a real, biological graph edge, not imagined.

Ref: Roberts et al. (2018), Psychoneuroendocrinology
💬

Inattentive Presentation

Girls are more likely to present with inattentive-type ADHD (daydreaming, difficulty following through, losing things) rather than hyperactive-impulsive type. Teachers and parents often miss it because "she's quiet" doesn't match the stereotype. The node exists — it's just labeled differently.

Ref: Biederman et al. (2002), American Journal of Psychiatry
😰

Internalized Shame

Without a diagnosis, women internalize their struggles as personal failures: "I'm lazy," "I can't keep up," "everyone else manages." This shame loop is often the most damaging downstream effect of late diagnosis. The graph path: undiagnosed ADHD → repeated failure → internalized shame → anxiety/depression → further masking → exhaustion.

Breaking this cycle starts with naming it
🤰

Pregnancy & Postpartum

Many women discover their ADHD during or after pregnancy, when the structure of work is removed and executive demands shift dramatically. Medication decisions during pregnancy require careful risk-benefit analysis with a specialist. Postpartum hormone shifts can severely amplify symptoms.

Discuss medication options with a perinatal psychiatrist
🔍

Getting Assessed

Seek providers who understand ADHD in women specifically. Standard screening tools were normed on male children and may miss your presentation. Ask for: comprehensive interview, multiple rating scales, assessment of compensatory strategies, and screening for comorbid anxiety/depression. A diagnosis is a graph edge that connects decades of "why" to an answer.

Relationships

ADHD doesn't just affect you — it affects every connection in your graph

Relationships have dependency edges too. Understanding how ADHD patterns play out in partnerships, friendships, parenting, and work relationships turns conflict into navigation.

The Parent-Child Dynamic in Partnerships

graph: forgotten_task → partner_picks_up_slack → resentment → nagging → shame → withdrawal → more_forgotten_tasks

This is the most common ADHD relationship cycle, described by Melissa Orlov as the "parent-child dynamic." The non-ADHD partner takes on more responsibility, begins to feel like a parent, builds resentment, and starts criticizing — which triggers RSD in the ADHD partner, causing withdrawal and further disengagement. It's a feedback loop, and both people feel trapped.

ADHD partner often feels:

Criticized, controlled, shame, "nothing I do is good enough," defensive, like a child being scolded.

Non-ADHD partner often feels:

Exhausted, alone, unappreciated, like a parent, resentful, unable to rely on their partner.

Breaking the cycle: Both partners need to see the graph. It's not "you're lazy" or "you're controlling" — it's a system with identifiable edges that can be rewired.

💑

Romantic Relationships

ADHD hyperfocus in early dating creates intense connection — then "fades." It's not lost interest; it's the brain's novelty-seeking system normalizing. Explaining this to your partner as a neurological pattern (not a character flaw) changes everything. External systems (shared calendars, check-in rituals) replace the need for willpower.

Ref: Orlov — The ADHD Effect on Marriage
👨‍👩‍👧

Parenting with ADHD

Parenting demands sustained executive function — the exact thing ADHD limits. Morning routines, homework supervision, emotional co-regulation: each requires the cognitive resources ADHD brains struggle to generate consistently. External scaffolding (visual schedules, timers, anchor routines) isn't lazy parenting — it's smart systems design.

Your child's routine is a graph you can optimize
🤝

Friendships

ADHD friendship challenges: forgetting to reply, missing events, intense-then-absent patterns, dominating conversations, interrupting. Most friends don't know it's ADHD — they just feel deprioritized. Naming the pattern and creating systems (recurring calendar invites, text reminders) preserves relationships that matter.

Upstream: working_memory → forgotten_reply → friend_feels_ignored
💼

Workplace Relationships

Missed deadlines, disorganized handoffs, blurting in meetings, difficulty with "boring" admin tasks that affect colleagues downstream. Understanding your blast radius at work — which teammates depend on your output — and building systems (shared trackers, brief daily syncs) turns a reputation problem into a reliability system.

Map your work dependency graph explicitly
